# Journal Profile: Academic Medicine

## Journal Identity

- **Full name**: Academic Medicine
- **Abbreviation**: Acad Med
- **Publisher**: Wolters Kluwer (Association of American Medical Colleges, AAMC)
- **ISSN**: 1040-2446 (print), 1938-808X (online)
- **Frequency**: Monthly (12 issues/year)
- **Impact Factor**: ~8 (JCR 2024), ranked 1st among medical education journals
- **Open Access**: Hybrid (optional OA with APC)
- **Acceptance rate**: ~10-12% for Research Reports
- **Peer review**: Double-blind; dedicated methodological and educational reviewer

## Abstract Requirements

**Structured abstract for Research Reports, 300 words maximum:**

```
Purpose: [Why this study was conducted — knowledge gap, problem addressed]
Method: [Study design, setting, participants, data collection, analysis approach]
Results: [Key quantitative or qualitative findings with effect sizes/themes]
Conclusions: [Main conclusion — 1-2 sentences answering the Purpose]
```

**For Innovation Reports**: Unstructured, 150 words maximum; summarize the problem, innovation, and early outcomes.

**Critical**: Academic Medicine uses 4-heading structured abstract (Purpose/Method/Results/Conclusions). "Purpose" is the first heading — it must articulate the specific gap being addressed.

## Required Journal-Specific Elements

### 1. Supplemental Digital Content (SDC)

- Appendices, additional tables, survey instruments, or detailed methods
- Referenced in-text as "Supplemental Digital Content 1"
- Uploaded as separate files during submission

### 2. Funding Statement

- Required for all submissions; state "None reported" if no funding

### 3. Ethical Approval

- IRB or equivalent ethics approval required (or exemption documented)
- Include institution name and determination (approved/exempt/not applicable)

### 4. Author Contribution Statement

- Required for all multi-author submissions
- Free-text format (not CRediT taxonomy)

## Required Sections (Research Report)

1. **Introduction** — 2-3 paragraphs; explicitly state conceptual or theoretical framework; end with research question(s) or objectives
2. **Method**
   - Study Design: specify paradigm (quantitative, qualitative, mixed-methods)
   - Setting and Participants: institution(s), sampling strategy, timeframe
   - Data Collection: instruments, procedures, response rates
   - Data Analysis: statistical tests or qualitative coding approach; software identified
   - Ethics: IRB determination
3. **Results** — present findings aligned with research questions; begin with response rate and sample description
4. **Discussion** — begin with key findings; address implications for medical education practice or policy; include limitations paragraph
5. **Conclusions** — brief (1-2 sentences)

### Required Sections (Innovation Report)

1. **Problem** — what gap in education prompted the innovation
2. **Approach** — description of the innovation (program, curriculum, tool)
3. **Outcomes** — early evaluation data (formal research not required)
4. **Next Steps** — plans for future evaluation or dissemination

## Citation Style

- AMA (American Medical Association) citation style
- Numbered sequentially; superscript numbers in text
- List first 6 authors, then "et al."
- Journal abbreviations per NLM catalog
- Format: Author(s). Title. *J Abbrev*. Year;Vol(Issue):Pages. doi:

## Statistical Reporting

Academic Medicine follows ICMJE and AAMC editorial standards:
- Report exact P values to 2 decimal places (e.g., P = .04); use P < .001 below that threshold.
- 95% CI required for primary outcomes in quantitative studies.
- Effect sizes (Cohen's d, odds ratio, relative risk) with CI for intervention studies.
- Report response rates with numerator/denominator (n/N, %).
- For qualitative studies: no quantitative statistical requirements; follow established qualitative reporting standards (SRQR preferred).
- Mixed-methods: report quantitative and qualitative components separately per their respective conventions.
- Survey studies: report response rate, demographic comparison of responders vs. non-responders if possible.
- Statistical software and version identified in Method section.
- Sample size justification recommended for quantitative studies.

## Figures

- **Maximum 5 figures/tables combined** for Research Reports
- **Resolution**: 300 DPI minimum
- **Format**: TIFF, EPS, or high-resolution PDF
- **Color**: Free for online; encouraged for clarity
- **Supplemental Digital Content**: Unlimited additional tables/figures as SDC
- **Conceptual framework diagrams**: highly valued in Introduction or Methods

## Common Rejection Reasons

1. **No conceptual or theoretical framework** — Academic Medicine expects educational research to be grounded in theory (e.g., self-determination theory, situated learning, Kirkpatrick model)
2. **Single-institution study with limited generalizability** — multi-site studies preferred; single-site acceptable if theory-driven with clear mechanisms
3. **Descriptive only without explanatory depth** — "what" without "why" or "how" is insufficient
4. **Innovation Report submitted as Research Report** — novel programs without rigorous evaluation belong in Innovation Reports
5. **Survey study with low response rate** — response rates below 60% require strong justification and non-response analysis
6. **Overclaiming from small samples** — qualify findings appropriately
7. **Missing theoretical lens for qualitative work** — must articulate methodology (grounded theory, phenomenology, etc.)

## Positioning

Academic Medicine is appropriate when:
- Theory-grounded educational research with validated outcome measures
- Large-scale multi-institutional educational studies (medical school, residency, CME)
- Health workforce, diversity/equity/inclusion research with national-level data
- AI in medical education studies (LLM performance, AI tutoring, automated assessment) with clear educational outcomes
- Innovation in curriculum or assessment backed by preliminary evaluation data

**Compared to Medical Education (ASME/Wiley)**:
- Academic Medicine favors US-based and AAMC-affiliated institutional research
- Medical Education has a stronger international/European presence
- Medical Education requires shorter manuscripts (3000 words) and emphasizes conceptual novelty
- Academic Medicine accepts Innovation Reports (no equivalent in Medical Education)

**Compared to Medical Teacher (AMEE/Taylor & Francis)**:
- Medical Teacher emphasizes practical guides ("Twelve Tips", AMEE Guides)
- Academic Medicine favors rigorous research methodology over practical how-to articles
- Medical Teacher has broader health professions scope (nursing, pharmacy, dentistry)
- Academic Medicine focuses primarily on physician education and academic medicine workforce

Not appropriate for: purely technical AI methodology papers, basic science without educational context, clinical outcomes research without educational intervention, descriptive program evaluations without evaluation framework.
