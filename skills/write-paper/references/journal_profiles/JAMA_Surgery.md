# Journal Profile: JAMA Surgery

## Journal Identity

- **Full name**: JAMA Surgery
- **Abbreviation**: JAMA Surg
- **Publisher**: American Medical Association (AMA)
- **ISSN**: 2168-6254 (print), 2168-6262 (online)
- **Frequency**: Monthly (12 issues/year)
- **Impact Factor**: ~16 (JCR 2023), ranked among top surgical journals
- **Open Access**: Hybrid (optional OA with APC ~$5,000)
- **Acceptance rate**: ~8-10% for Original Investigations
- **Peer review**: Single-blind; statistical editor review on all accepted manuscripts

## Abstract Requirements

**Structured abstract, 350 words maximum:**

```
Importance: [Why this question matters — clinical/surgical impact, knowledge gap]
Objective: [Specific aims — 1 sentence]
Design, Setting, and Participants: [Study design, setting, eligibility criteria,
    dates of data collection, follow-up duration]
Intervention(s) [if applicable]: [Description of surgical interventions/exposures]
Main Outcomes and Measures: [Primary and secondary outcomes defined]
Results: [Number enrolled/analyzed, demographic summary, primary results
    with effect sizes, CIs, and exact P values]
Conclusions and Relevance: [Main conclusion — 1-2 sentences answering
    the Objective; surgical/clinical relevance]
```

**Critical**: JAMA Surgery uses the 7-heading JAMA Network structured abstract. The "Importance" heading is the first thing editors read and must justify why the surgical community needs this study.

## Required Journal-Specific Elements

### 1. Key Points Box

Placed immediately before the abstract. Three components:

- **Question**: One sentence framing the research question.
- **Findings**: One sentence with the main quantitative result (effect size + CI).
- **Meaning**: One sentence stating surgical/clinical relevance.

Example:
```
Key Points

Question  Does minimally invasive esophagectomy reduce postoperative
pulmonary complications compared with open esophagectomy?

Findings  In this randomized clinical trial of 502 patients, minimally
invasive esophagectomy was associated with significantly fewer pulmonary
complications (12.5% vs 22.8%; relative risk, 0.55 [95% CI, 0.38-0.79]).

Meaning  Minimally invasive esophagectomy may reduce pulmonary morbidity
and should be considered the preferred approach in experienced centers.
```

### 2. Article Information

- Author affiliations with department, institution, city
- Corresponding author email
- Author contributions (CRediT format accepted)
- Conflict of interest disclosures (ICMJE form required from all authors)
- Funding/support statement
- Data sharing statement

## Required Sections (Original Investigation)

1. **Introduction** — brief (2-3 paragraphs); end with explicit objective
2. **Methods**
   - Ethics: IRB approval with institution name + approval number
   - Study Design and Participants: inclusion/exclusion, recruitment dates
   - Interventions or Surgical Procedures (if applicable): standardized technique description
   - Outcomes: primary and secondary clearly defined (Clavien-Dindo for complications when relevant)
   - Statistical Analysis: software, tests, sample size calculation, handling of multiplicity
3. **Results** — begin with participant flow and baseline characteristics
4. **Discussion** — 4-5 paragraphs; begin with key findings summary, end with Limitations paragraph
5. **Conclusions** — brief (1-2 sentences, matching abstract conclusion)

## Statistical Reporting

JAMA Surgery follows JAMA Network statistical standards:
- Report exact P values to 2 decimal places (e.g., P = .03); use P < .001 below that threshold.
- 95% CI required for all primary and key secondary outcomes.
- Effect sizes with units (absolute risk difference, hazard ratio, odds ratio, NNT).
- Sample size justification required in Methods (power calculation with assumptions stated).
- Report both intention-to-treat and per-protocol analyses for RCTs.
- Multiple comparisons: pre-specify primary outcome; correct for multiplicity or declare secondary analyses as exploratory.
- Means with SDs for normally distributed data; medians with IQRs for skewed data.
- Statistical software and version must be identified in Methods.
- For surgical outcome studies: Clavien-Dindo classification, 30-day and 90-day outcomes preferred.

## Figures

- **Maximum 5 figures/tables combined** — plan carefully
- **Resolution**: 300 DPI minimum (600 DPI for line art)
- **Format**: EPS, PDF, or TIFF preferred
- **Color**: Free for online; print charges may apply
- **Supplementary material**: Unlimited; include as eSupplement (eTables, eFigures, eMethods)
- **Figure panels**: Label A, B, C; legends must be self-explanatory
- **CONSORT/STROBE flow**: counts as one figure

## Common Rejection Reasons

1. **Insufficient novelty or sample size** — JAMA Surgery expects large multicenter or population-based studies for clinical outcomes research
2. **Missing Key Points box** — required at submission; auto-reject without it
3. **Single-center retrospective without clear advantage** — must offer unique data or technique not available in registries
4. **Lack of clinical endpoints** — surrogate endpoints alone are insufficient; patient-centered outcomes preferred
5. **Methodological concerns** — inadequate risk adjustment, selection bias, or missing variables
6. **Overclaiming** — causal language in observational studies
7. **AI/imaging studies without surgical relevance** — technical AI papers should go to specialty imaging journals

---

## Cover Letter

Must include:
- Statement of novelty (1-2 sentences: what this adds to surgical practice)
- Statement that manuscript is not under consideration elsewhere
- Statement that all authors approved the final version
- Disclosure of any related manuscripts (submitted or in press)
- Suggested and opposed reviewers (optional but recommended)
- If transfer from JAMA: mention transfer and include previous decision letter

## JAMA Network Transfer

JAMA Surgery accepts transfers from JAMA. If rejected from JAMA Surgery, authors can transfer to:
- **JAMA Network Open** — all fields, multidisciplinary OA
- Other JAMA Network specialty journals (if scope matches)

Transfer preserves reviews. Editors make independent decisions.

---

## Positioning

JAMA Surgery is appropriate when:
- Large RCT or multicenter prospective cohort comparing surgical techniques or strategies
- Population-based study (NSQIP, NIS, NCDB) with novel surgical outcome findings
- Practice-changing surgical quality/safety research
- Systematic review/meta-analysis of surgical interventions with clinical implications
- AI/technology study with clear impact on surgical decision-making

Not appropriate for: small single-center case series, technical notes without outcome data, non-surgical imaging AI, narrow subspecialty findings better suited to organ-specific journals.

## AI Writing Disclosure Policy
- **Requirement level:** Required
- **Permitted scope:** Language editing only — AI/LLM tools may be used for editing, grammar, and language improvement but cannot be listed as authors; AI must not generate scientific content, data analysis, or conclusions; authors bear full responsibility for all content including any AI-assisted text
- **Disclosure location:** Methods + Cover letter — describe the AI tool name, version, and how it was used in the Methods section; also disclose in the cover letter; ICMJE-compliant disclosure required
- **AI-generated images:** Discouraged — if used, authors must provide a clear description of the content created and the AI tool name, version, and manufacturer; AI-generated or AI-manipulated images must be disclosed; fabrication/falsification policies apply
- **Policy URL:** https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamasurgery/pages/instructions-for-authors
