# Journal Profile: Annals of Surgery

## Journal Identity

- **Full name**: Annals of Surgery
- **Abbreviation**: Ann Surg
- **Publisher**: Wolters Kluwer
- **ISSN**: 0003-4932 (print), 1528-1140 (online)
- **Frequency**: Monthly (12 issues/year)
- **Impact Factor**: ~8 (JCR 2023), historically the most prestigious general surgery journal
- **Open Access**: Hybrid (optional OA with APC)
- **Acceptance rate**: ~10-12%
- **Peer review**: Single-blind; rigorous statistical and methodological review

## Abstract Requirements

**Structured abstract, 250 words maximum:**

```
Objective: [Specific aim of the study — 1-2 sentences]
Summary Background Data: [Brief context — what is known and what gap exists]
Methods: [Study design, setting, participants, interventions, main outcomes,
    statistical approach]
Results: [Key quantitative findings with effect sizes, CIs, P values]
Conclusions: [Main conclusion — 1-2 sentences; clinical/surgical implications]
```

**Critical**: Annals of Surgery uses a 5-heading format with "Summary Background Data" (not "Background" or "Introduction"). The "Objective" comes first, followed by context. This ordering emphasizes the research question before background.

## Required Journal-Specific Elements

### 1. Mini-Abstract

A 50-word unstructured summary for the table of contents. Must capture the main finding in plain language accessible to a broad surgical audience.

### 2. Article Information

- Author affiliations with department, institution, city, country
- Corresponding author with full contact details
- Author contributions statement
- Conflict of interest disclosures
- Funding/support statement
- IRB/Ethics approval statement
- Data availability statement

---

## Required Sections (Original Article)

1. **Introduction** — 2-3 paragraphs; state knowledge gap; end with clear study objective
2. **Methods**
   - Ethics: IRB approval with institution name
   - Study Design and Participants: inclusion/exclusion criteria, dates, setting
   - Surgical Technique/Intervention (if applicable): standardized description
   - Outcome Measures: primary and secondary defined a priori; use validated scoring systems (Clavien-Dindo, CCI)
   - Statistical Analysis: methods, software, sample size justification, handling of missing data
3. **Results** — begin with patient flow (CONSORT/STROBE as applicable); baseline characteristics table; then outcomes in order of primary → secondary
4. **Discussion** — begin with principal findings; compare with literature; discuss mechanisms; address limitations in dedicated paragraph
5. **Conclusions** — brief paragraph (not just one sentence); state clinical implications

---

## Statistical Reporting

- Report exact P values (e.g., P = 0.03); use P < 0.001 for very small values.
- 95% CI required for all primary outcomes and key comparisons.
- Effect sizes: OR, RR, HR, mean difference with CI — not P values alone.
- Multivariable analysis: report adjusted estimates; specify covariates and rationale.
- Sample size/power calculation required for RCTs and recommended for others.
- Propensity score methods: describe matching algorithm, caliper, balance assessment.
- Time-to-event: Kaplan-Meier with log-rank; Cox regression with proportional hazards assumption check.
- Multiple comparisons: Bonferroni or FDR correction; declare secondary/exploratory.
- Missing data: report proportion; describe imputation method if used.
- Statistical software and version required.
- For surgical studies: report 30-day and 90-day outcomes; use standardized complication grading.

## Figures

- **Maximum 6 figures/tables combined** for Original Articles
- **Resolution**: 300 DPI minimum; 600 DPI for line art
- **Format**: TIFF, EPS, or PDF
- **Color**: Available at no charge
- **Supplementary Digital Content (SDC)**: unlimited; hosted separately as SDC files
- **Figure quality**: Professional quality expected; flow diagrams follow CONSORT/PRISMA
- **Intraoperative images**: high-quality, annotated, with patient consent documented

## Common Rejection Reasons

1. **Insufficient sample or single-center limitation** — Annals favors multicenter, registry-based, or large population studies
2. **Incremental findings** — must advance surgical practice, not merely confirm known outcomes
3. **Inadequate comparator or control group** — surgical studies need well-matched comparisons
4. **Methodological weakness** — selection bias in retrospective studies without adequate adjustment (propensity, IV, etc.)
5. **Scope mismatch** — overly subspecialized studies may fit organ-specific journals better
6. **Poor outcome definition** — non-standardized complication grading, unclear primary endpoint
7. **Overclaiming from observational data** — causal language without causal design

## Positioning

Annals of Surgery is appropriate when:
- Large multicenter RCT or prospective surgical cohort (N > 500 preferred)
- Registry or administrative database study (NSQIP, NIS, NCDB) with novel findings
- Practice-changing meta-analysis of surgical interventions
- Landmark surgical technique comparison (MIS vs open, robotic vs laparoscopic)
- Quality improvement study with generalizable implications

Not appropriate for: pilot feasibility studies, small case series, non-surgical imaging or AI papers without surgical decision impact, highly subspecialized studies (use organ-specific journals: HPB, Diseases of the Colon & Rectum, etc.).
