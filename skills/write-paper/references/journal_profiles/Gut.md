# Journal Profile: Gut (BSG/BMJ)

## Basic Information

- **Publisher:** BMJ Publishing Group (British Society of Gastroenterology)
- **Impact Factor:** ~26 (2023–2024)
- **Scope:** Original research and reviews in gastroenterology and hepatology, covering the entire GI tract, liver, biliary system, and pancreas
- **Sister journals:** Gut Microbes, Frontline Gastroenterology (clinical education)
- **Open Access:** Hybrid (BMJ Open Access option)
- **Language:** British English

## Abstract Requirements

**Structured abstract, 250 words maximum:**

```
Objective: [What was studied — 1–2 sentences]
Design: [Study design, setting, participants]
Results: [Key results with statistics, 95% CI]
Conclusion: [Main conclusion and clinical relevance]
```

**Significance of this study** — 3-part box required immediately after the abstract:

```
What is already known on this subject?
• [2–3 bullet points]

What are the new findings?
• [2–3 bullet points]

How might it impact on clinical practice in the foreseeable future?
• [2–3 bullet points]
```

This is the first element editors and reviewers evaluate. Must be concise and compelling.

## Style Requirements

### British English

Use British English throughout:
- colour (not color), characterise (not characterize), favour (not favor)
- analyse (not analyze), centre (not center), haemorrhage (not hemorrhage)
- focussed (acceptable), oesophagus (not esophagus)

### Statistical Reporting

- Report exact p-values to 3 decimal places (P = .034); values < .001 reported as P < .001
- 95% confidence intervals in parentheses
- Effect sizes with units
- Hazard ratios, odds ratios, or risk ratios with 95% CI
- Statistical software and version identified in Methods

### Number Formatting

- Decimal separator: period
- P values: italic P with space (P = .034)
- Percentages: [value]% without space

## Required Sections (Original Research)

1. **Introduction** — brief (300–400 words); state knowledge gap and explicit aim
2. **Methods**
   - Ethics: approval number + consent/waiver statement
   - Study design and setting
   - Participants: inclusion/exclusion, recruitment
   - Data collection and outcomes
   - Statistical analysis: software, tests, multiplicity correction
3. **Results** — primary outcome first, then secondary; no interpretation
4. **Discussion** — typically 4–5 paragraphs
   - Strengths and limitations as a subsection
   - Comparison with existing literature
5. **Conclusion** — brief final paragraph

## Figure Requirements

- **Resolution:** 300 DPI minimum (600 DPI for line art)
- **Format:** TIFF, EPS, or high-resolution JPEG
- **Dimensions:** Single column 84 mm, double column 174 mm
- **Colour figures:** Free online; print colour charged
- **Multi-panel figures:** Label A, B, C; count as one figure
- **Supplementary figures:** Unlimited; encouraged for additional data

## Common Rejection Reasons

1. **Incremental contribution** — Gut expects studies that advance understanding or change practice; replication of known findings in a new population is insufficient
2. **Missing "Significance of this study" box** — desk rejection if absent or poorly written
3. **Underpowered study** — single-centre studies with < 200 participants rarely accepted for original research
4. **Scope mismatch** — purely imaging or purely basic science without clear GI clinical relevance
5. **British English violations** — minor but flagged by reviewers; "esophagus" instead of "oesophagus" signals unfamiliarity with the journal
6. **Statistical deficiencies** — missing CIs, inappropriate multiplicity correction
7. **Overclaiming** — Gut editors sensitive to "first," "novel," "unique" without justification

## Cover Letter Tips

- Address to: "Dear Editor"
- State the manuscript type
- Include the "Significance of this study" summary (editors read this first)
- Confirm no dual submission, author approval, conflicts disclosed
- Suggest 3–5 potential reviewers (names, institutions, emails) — strongly encouraged
- Mention relevance to BSG/GI community

## Positioning

Gut is well-suited for:
- Large multicentre GI/hepatology studies (European or international preferred)
- AI-assisted endoscopy studies with clinical validation (polyp detection, Barrett's)
- IBD and GI oncology clinical research with substantial cohorts
- Microbiome studies linking to GI disease with mechanistic insight
- Imaging studies (CT colonography, MR enterography) addressing clinical GI questions

Compared with:
- **Gastroenterology (AGA):** Similar tier but American focus; Gut favours European/international cohorts and British English style. Gut stronger in IBD and endoscopy AI; Gastroenterology stronger in motility and AGA guideline-related work.
- **Journal of Hepatology (EASL):** Higher IF (~33) but liver-only scope. Pure hepatology studies go to J Hepatol; mixed GI-liver or endoscopy-related liver studies can fit Gut.

Not well-suited for: small single-centre series (<50 patients), purely basic science without translational bridge, imaging studies with no GI clinical question.

## AI Writing Disclosure Policy
- **Requirement level:** Required
- **Permitted scope:** Moderate/Permissive — BMJ Group policy permits AI for all manuscript preparation tasks (writing, editing, analysis support); AI cannot be listed as author; authors are accountable for all content including AI-generated text; follows ICMJE + COPE guidance
- **Disclosure location:** Methods + Acknowledgments — must disclose AI tool name, version, and how it was used; applies to all content types (text, images, data, diagrams)
- **AI-generated images:** Must be declared — AI-generated or AI-manipulated images and diagrams must be explicitly disclosed
- **Policy URL:** https://www.bmj.com/company/the-bmjs-position-on-ai-and-large-language-models/
